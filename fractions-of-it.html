🎂 Fractions: "of a number" vs "of IT"

A common mix-up that trips up many students — let's fix it once and for all!

🔑 What does "of" mean in maths?

"of" = multiply
Whenever you see "fraction of something", you multiply the fraction by that something.

"2/3 of 12" means 2/3 × 12 = 8
⚠️
The tricky word: "IT"
"It" refers to the amount already mentioned. You must find out what "it" is first before you can take a fraction of it.

Compare these two sentences:

Example A

Mary has 2/3 L of juice.

Bar model — the whole = 1 L
2/3 L
1/3 L

We know exactly: 2/3 of 1 litre = 0.667 L.
No mystery — it's a fixed amount.

Example B

Mary drank 2/3 of it.

What is "it"? — We need to know first!
If it = 3L:
2 L
If it = 6L:
4 L

The amount changes depending on what "it" is.
You must identify the whole first!

🧠 Memory Trick

"2/3 L" → already a number (2/3 of ONE litre)
"2/3 of it" → ask yourself: "2/3 of WHAT?" → find "it" first → then multiply

🎂 The Problem

John bought 4/5 kg of cake.
He gave away 1/3 of it.
How much cake did he have left?
🚨
The #1 mistake students make:
They see "4/5" and "1/3" and immediately subtract: 4/5 − 1/3.
But wait — "1/3 of it" is NOT the same as the number 1/3!

❌ Wrong Way vs ✅ Right Way

❌ WRONG

Treating "1/3 of it" as just the number 1/3 and subtracting:

4/5 − 1/3 = ?
12/15 − 5/15 = 7/15 kg ✗

This treats "1/3" as a separate amount of cake — but John didn't have 1/3 kg sitting somewhere else!

✅ RIGHT

"1/3 of it" = 1/3 of the cake John bought (4/5 kg):

1/3 × 4/5 = 4/15
given away = 4/15 kg

Then: 4/5 − 4/15 = 12/15 − 4/15 = 8/15 kg ✓

Reading the bar model:
• John's cake (4/5 kg) is split into 3 equal parts
• Each part = 4/5 ÷ 3 = 4/5 × 1/3 = 4/15 kg
• He gave away 1 part = 4/15 kg
• He kept 2 parts = 8/15 kg

🔢 Step-by-Step Working

1

Spot the key word: "of it"
"1/3 of it" → "it" refers to the 4/5 kg John bought.
So we need to find: 1/3 of 4/5 kg

1/3 of 4/5 = 1/3 × 4/5
2

Calculate how much was given away
Multiply the fractions (top × top, bottom × bottom):

1/3 × 4/5 = (1×4)/(3×5) = 4/15 kg
3

Subtract to find what's left
Change 4/5 to fifteenths: 4/5 = 12/15

12/15 − 4/15 = 8/15 kg
✓

Answer

John had 8/15 kg of cake left.
Why 4/5 − 1/3 is wrong:
4/5 is the total amount of cake. 1/3 is NOT a separate amount — it's a fraction of that cake.
Subtracting 1/3 from 4/5 would mean John had 1/3 kg of something else, which makes no sense in this problem!

✅ Quick Check — Which is it?

For each question, decide: do you subtract the fraction directly, or find "of it" first?

Q1. Sarah has a ribbon 3/4 m long. She cuts off 1/2 of it. How much did she cut?
Q2. A tank holds 5/6 L of water. Ali pours out 2/5 of it. How much is poured out?
Q3. Tom ran 3/5 km. Ben ran 1/3 km. How much further did Tom run?
Q4. A bag had 4/7 kg of flour. Mum used 3/4 of it for baking. How much flour was used?

🧠 The Golden Rule

📖 Read carefully!

Two separate amounts? → Add or Subtract them

e.g. "Tom has 3/5 km, Ben has 1/3 km → find difference"

Fraction "of it" / "of the"? → Multiply first

e.g. "gave away 1/3 of it" → 1/3 × (the amount)
